My background is a bit of an odd mix, and I think that’s worth explaining upfront.

I spent the first chunk of my career doing data and statistics work. I built risk models and credit scorecards at GM Financial, then moved into data science at Elevate Finance where I worked on machine learning and operational strategy. I liked the work, but what I kept finding myself doing in my off hours was something different entirely. I was running servers at home, wiring together self-hosted services, figuring out how to make things scale and talk to each other. The homelab started as a hobby and turned into an education. Eventually it became my career.

The shift to DevOps made sense to me because a lot of the same thinking applies. When I set up monitoring at Wells Fargo for their RSA authentication infrastructure, I approached it the same way I used to approach a modeling problem: what are the signals that actually matter, and how do you build something that tells you about a problem before it becomes a crisis? The statistics background has been genuinely useful in ways I didn’t expect.

At Digital River I spent six years building and maintaining infrastructure on AWS, running Kubernetes clusters, writing Terraform and Ansible to keep things consistent across environments, and building out CI/CD pipelines. I enjoy that kind of work. There’s something satisfying about taking a messy manual process and automating it cleanly, or figuring out why a system is behaving in a way it shouldn’t.

I like building things that work well and that other people can actually use. That’s true whether it’s a monitoring dashboard, a deployment pipeline, or something running on my homelab on a Saturday afternoon.

If you’d like to talk more about my background or have any questions, please don’t hesitate to reach out. I’m happy to get into specifics on anything.
